Comparing Two Populations

Samuel Burer

Last updated: September 14, 2016

Comparing Two Populations: Means

  • Suppose you want to estimate the difference \(\mu_1 - \mu_2\) of the means of two separate populations

  • You have obtained a random sample of each population

    • \(n_1 \ge 30\), \(\bar x_1\), \(s_1\)
    • \(n_2 \ge 30\), \(\bar x_2\), \(s_2\)

Comparing Two Populations: Means (cont’d)

A \(\beta\)% CI for \(\mu_1 - \mu_2\) is \[(\bar x_1 - \bar x_2) \pm c \sqrt{\frac{s_1^2}{n_1} + \frac{s_2^2}{n_2}}\] where \(c\) is the same as in the large-sample-size case

Comparing Two Populations: Proportions

  • Suppose you want to estimate the difference \(p_1 - p_2\) of the proportions of two separate populations

  • You have obtained a random sample of each population

    • \(\bar p_1\), \(n_1 \bar p_1 \ge 5\), \(n_1 (1 - \bar p_1) \ge 5\)
    • \(\bar p_2\), \(n_2 \bar p_2 \ge 5\), \(n_2 (1 - \bar p_2) \ge 5\)

Comparing Two Populations: Proportions (cont’d)

A \(\beta\)% CI for \(p_1 - p_2\) is \[(\bar p_1 - \bar p_2) \pm c \sqrt{\frac{\bar p_1 (1 - \bar p_1)}{n_1} + \frac{\bar p_2 (1 - \bar p_2)}{n_2}}\] where \(c\) is the same as in the large-sample-size case
