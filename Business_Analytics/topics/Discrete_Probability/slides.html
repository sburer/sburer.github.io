Outcomes and Probability

Imagine an uncertain situation with a number of possible outcomes, e.g.:

  • Rolling a fair die
    • outcomes 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6
  • Calling a random person as part of a political poll
    • outcomes Republican, Democrat, age 18-64, age 65+

Outcomes and Probability (cont’d)

The probability of a particular outcome is the likelihood that the outcome will occur

  • Measured on a scale from 0 to 1 (or 0% to 100%)
  • 0 means definitely will not happen
  • 1 means definitely will happen

E.g., for a fair die…

  • Probability of getting a 1 is 1/6
  • Probability of getting a 2 is 1/6
  • Etc.

Outcomes and Probability (cont’d)

To simplify, we will only investigate situations having MECE outcomes

  • ME = mutually exclusive = no two outcomes can occur together
  • CE = collectively exhaustive = at least one must occur

Probabilities of MECE outcomes add to 100%

Outcomes and Probability (cont’d)

Examples:

  • Rolling a fair die
    • 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6 are MECE
  • Calling a random person as part of a political poll
    • Republican and Age 65+ are not ME
    • Republican and Democrat are not CE

Random Variables

  • When an uncertain situation leads to numerical MECE outcomes, the uncertain quantity is called a random variable

  • The random variable is often denoted by a capital letter such as \(X\) or \(Y\)

Random Variables (cont’d)

  • Every random variable \(X\) is either discrete or continuous
    • discrete = \(X\) has distinct and separate outcomes
    • continuous = \(X\) can take on any value within some range of numbers
  • For now, we focus on DRVs (discrete random variables)

Random Variables (cont’d)

Example Random Variable

Probability Distribution

Let \(X\) be a DRV with \(n\) MECE outcomes

  • Each outcome is denoted \(x_i\) for \(i = 1,\ldots,n\)

  • \(P(X = x_i)\) equals the probability that \(X\) takes on value \(x_i\)

  • Taken together, the probabilities are called the probability distribution function (or PDF) of \(X\)

The PDF is often shown as a bar chart

Cumulative Distribution

Let \(X\) be a DRV with \(n\) MECE outcomes

  • \(P(X \le x_i)\) equals the probability that \(X\) takes on any value less than or equal to \(x_i\)

  • Taken together, these probabilities are called the cumulative distribution function (or CDF) of \(X\)

The CDF is best shown as a line chart overlaid on the PDF

  • starts at 0, increases left to right, ends at 1

Why bother with random variables?

  • Random variables are the “language of randomness”
  • Every DRV has a its own PDF and CDF
  • We can use these to answer questions about the probability of certain events

Summary Measures for DRVs

When talking about a DRV \(X\), we often describe two key characteristics of \(X\):

  1. Centrality = what are the most common/frequent/probable values of X, i.e., what is the “center” of \(X\)?

  2. Dispersion = how widely do the values of \(X\) deviate from its center?

Expected Value

The expected value \(E(X)\) of \(X\) is the average of all the possible values for \(X\), weighted by their probabilities:

\[E(X) = \sum_{i=1}^n P(X = x_i) \cdot x_i\]

  • Also called the mean
  • The most common measure of centrality

Expected Value (cont’d)

A common interpretation of expected value:

Expected value is the long-run average of the random variable.

That is, if \(X\) is repeated ad infinitum, then \(E(X)\) equals the average of the actual observed values of X

Standard Deviation

The standard deviation \(\sigma_X\) of \(X\) is a measure of how far, on average, the values of \(X\) deviate from its expected value, \(E(X)\)

\[\sigma_X = \sqrt{ \sum_{i=1}^n P(X = x_i) \cdot (x_i - E(X))^2 }\]

  • The bigger \(\sigma_X\), the bigger the deviation
  • The most common measure of dispersion
  • Variance: VAR(\(X\)) = \(\sigma_X^2\)

Standard Deviation (cont’d)

  • Std dev is in the same units as \(X\), and so we often talk about std dev rather than variance

  • By itself, however, std dev is sometimes difficult on its own

  • Often, we compare the std devs of two random variables \(X\) and \(Y\) as a measure of which has more “risk”
