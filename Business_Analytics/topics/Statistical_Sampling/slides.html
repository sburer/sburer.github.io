The Statistical Point of View

  • What is the average level of education in Iowa?
  • What is United Airlines’ on-time percentage?
  • What proportion of 25-34 year-old males enjoy “reality” shows like American Idol and Survivor?

These are questions about the underlying probability distributions

The Statistical Point of View (cont’d)

Something to think about…

  • You flip a coin 10 times
  • It comes up Heads each time
  • What is the probability that it will be Heads on the 11th flip?

The Statistical Point of View (cont’d)

  • We assume that, in any given situation, there is an underlying probability distribution (or random variable \(X\)) that determines the outcomes we’re interested in.

  • Statistics deduces properties of \(X\) from empirical knowledge of the real world.

Populations and Samples

  • Population = all items of interest for a particular situation, investigation, or decision

    • all adult males (height)
    • all incoming calls to call-center (call length)
    • all citizens (voting)
    • all white blood cells in your body (medical condition)
    • all light bulbs from manufacturing process (burn time)

  • Definition depends on situation

    • Up to you to define relevant population

Populations and Samples (cont’d)

  • Sample = a subset of the population

    • adult males in this class
    • randomly monitored call-center calls
    • citizens who vote by absentee ballot
    • a blood sample used for medical analysis
    • sample of 20 light bulbs used for testing

Populations and Samples (cont’d)

  • Usually want a random sample

    • I.e., a sample collected such that every member of the population is equally likely to be selected

  • Sample design involves specifying a procedure for collecting a sample

    • E.g., subjective sampling, probabilistic sampling
    • It is a real challenge to get a truly representative sample

Populations and Samples (cont’d)

  • Why work with samples?

    • Often, it is inconvenient (or impossible) to deal with the entire population
    • Samples are more manageable

The Setup

  • Let \(X\) be the random variable that underlies your population of interest

  • You would like to learn as much about \(X\) as possible

  • For example, \(X\) has its own PDF, CDF, mean, standard deviation, etc. What are they?

    • Define \(\mu = E(X)\) = expected value of X
    • Define \(\sigma\) = standard deviation of X

The Setup (cont’d)

Examples

  • \(X\) = height of an adult male
  • \(X\) = length of call to call center
  • \(X\) = 1 if citizen votes Republican, 0 otherwise
  • \(X\) = 1 if white blood cell has medical condition, 0 otherwise
  • \(X\) = burn time of light bulb

The Setup (cont’d)

Examples

  • What is the average adult male height? What is the standard deviation? If Joe Smith is 71.5 inches tall, what percentage of adult males are taller than Joe?

    • \(\mu\), \(\sigma\), \(P(X \ge 71.5)\)?

  • What proportion of citizens vote Republican?

    • \(\mu\)? (Note: \(\mu\) often called \(p\) to indicate proportion)

  • Government rule: The advertised burn time must hold for 95% of all light bulbs

    • What \(x\) gives \(P(X \ge x)\) = 0.95?

The Setup (cont’d)

  • Imagine that you

    • randomly sample \(n\) members of the population, i.e., your sample size is \(n\)

    • measure \(X\) for each member

  • Let \(x_i\) = the \(i\)-th member’s observed numerical value

  • Do the \(x_i\)’s tell you much about \(X\)?

    • PDF or CDF?
    • \(\mu\) or \(\sigma\)?
    • As a matter of fact, yes…

Analyzing the Sample

Once you have observed a random sample of size \(n\), how can you make conclusions about \(X\)? Recall:

  • Relative histogram
  • Cumulative relative histogram

Analyzing the Sample (cont’d)

Recall:

  • Observed sample mean \(\bar x = (x_1 + x_2 + \cdots + x_n)/n\)

  • Observed sample standard deviation \[s = \sqrt{ \frac{(x_1 - \bar x)^2 + (x_2 - \bar x)^2 + \cdots + (x_n - \bar x)^2}{n-1} }\]

Analyzing the Sample (cont’d)

Characteristic of \(X\) Estimate
PDF Relative histogram
CDF Cumulative relative histogram
\(\mu\) Observed sample mean \(\bar x\)
\(\sigma\) Observed sample std dev \(s\)

Theory of Sampling

Key theoretical point:

  • Our intuition suggests that the bigger \(n\) is, the better the estimates of \(X\) will be

  • Theory confirms this is true

Theory of Sampling (cont’d)

For example…

  • Assume \(n\) is large (say, \(n \ge 30\))
  • The sample mean \(\bar x\) follows a normal distribution (even if \(X\) is not normal itself)
    • Its mean is \(\mu\)
    • Its std dev is \(\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}\)

Theory of Sampling (cont’d)

Standard error of the mean = \(\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}\)

  • The standard error tells us how far \(\bar x\) is from the \(\mu\)

  • E.g.

    • We can say, “95% of the time, \(\bar x\) falls within 1.960 std errors of \(\mu\)”
    • We know that, as \(n\) goes up, error goes down

Confidence Intervals

  • \(\bar x\) is an estimate of \(\mu\)
  • But how good an estimate is it?
  • Confidence intervals help us answer this question…

Confidence Intervals (cont’d)

Given random sample with

  • large sample size \(n \ge 30\)
  • observed sample mean \(\bar x\)
  • observed sample std dev \(s\)

A 95% confidence interval for \(\mu\) is \[ \bar x \pm 1.96 \frac{s}{\sqrt{n}} \quad \text{or} \quad \left[ \bar x - 1.96 \frac{s}{\sqrt{n}}, \bar x + 1.96 \frac{s}{\sqrt{n}} \right] \]

Confidence Intervals (cont’d)

More generally, a \(\beta\)% confidence interval for \(\mu\) is \[ \bar x \pm c \cdot \frac{s}{\sqrt{n}} \quad \text{or} \quad \left[ \bar x - c \cdot \frac{s}{\sqrt{n}}, \bar x + c \cdot \frac{s}{\sqrt{n}} \right] \]

where \(c = \text{NORM.S.INV}(1-(1- \beta/100)/2)\)

Confidence Intervals (cont’d)

Most common \(c\) values

\(\beta\)% \(c\)
90% 1.645
95% 1.960
98% 2.326
99% 2.576

Confidence Intervals (cont’d)

  • Technical interpretation of a \(\beta\)% CI

    • If we take many samples of size \(n\) and construct all the corresponding \(\beta\)% CI’s, then about \(\beta\)% of those CI’s will contain \(\mu\)

  • Common interpretation of a \(\beta\)% CI

    • “We are \(\beta\)% sure that \(\mu\) is contained in the CI.”

CI’s for Small Sample Size

Given random sample with

  • small sample size \(n < 30\)
  • observed sample mean \(\bar x\)
  • observed sample std dev \(s\)

We can still construct a CI for \(\mu\), but we need an additional assumption

  • underlying distribution of \(X\) is normally distributed

CI’s for Small Sample Size (cont’d)

A \(\beta\)% confidence interval for \(\mu\) is \[ \bar x \pm c \cdot \frac{s}{\sqrt{n}} \]

where \(c = \text{T.INV}(1-(1-\beta/100)/2, n-1)\)

CI’s for Proportions

  • Imagine that \(X\) is a “0 or 1” random variable

    • yes or no, success or failure

  • We are are often interested in \(p\), the proportion of the population having value 1

    • Interpretation: \(P(X = 1) = p\)

  • \(p\) is the population proportion

  • \(\bar p\) is the observed sample proportion

\[ \bar p = \frac{\text{number of successes}}{n} \]

CI’s for Proportions (cont’d)

If \(n \bar p \ge 5\) and \(n(1 - \bar p) \ge 5\), then a \(\beta\)% CI for \(p\) is \[ \bar p \pm c \sqrt{\frac{\bar p(1 - \bar p)}{n}} \] where \(c\) is the same as in the large-sample-size case

Experimental Design: Means

  • Consider estimating \(\mu\). How many observations \(n\) are needed to obtain a \(\beta\)% confidence interval for a specified tolerance level \(L\)?

  • I.e., what \(n\) will give a CI of the form \(\bar x \pm L\)?

Experimental Design: Means (cont’d)

  • Suppose you have an observed sample std dev \(s\) gotten from a small pilot sample

  • Then the required sample size \(n\) to achieve a \(\beta\)% CI for \(\mu\) with tolerance level \(L\) is \[n = \frac{c^2 s^2}{L^2}\] where \(c\) is the same as in the large-sample-size case

Experimental Design: Means (cont’d)

Also

  • If \(n\) is fractional, then round up to the nearest whole number

  • If \(n < 30\), then increase \(n\) to 30

Exercise: Heights (cont’d)

Experimental Design: Proportions

  • Consider estimating \(p\). How many observations \(n\) are needed to obtain a \(\beta\)% confidence interval for a specified tolerance level \(L\)?

  • I.e., what \(n\) will give a CI of the form \(\bar p \pm L\)?

  • In this case, \(L\) is a decimal between 0 and 1

Experimental Design: Proportions (cont’d)

  • The required sample size \(n\) to achieve a \(\beta\)% CI for \(p\) with tolerance level \(L\) is \[n = \frac{c^2}{4 L^2}\] where \(c\) is the same as in the large-sample-size case

Polling Example

  • In estimating the proportion of the population that approves of the President’s performance, how many people should be polled to provide a 95% confidence interval with error of 3 percentage points?

  • Want to be able to say something like: “44% of Americans approve of the job the President is doing, plus or minus 3 percentage points”

  • Apply formula with \(c = 1.960\) and \(L = 0.03\): \[n = 1.960^2 / (4 \cdot 0.03^2 ) = 1067.1 \approx 1068\]
