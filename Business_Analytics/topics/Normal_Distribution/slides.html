Important Properties

  • Imagine a normal random variable \(X\) with mean \(\mu\) and std dev \(\sigma\)
  • We write \(X \sim N(\mu, \sigma)\)

Important Properties (cont’d)

 

Important Properties (cont’d)

  • \(P(\mu - \sigma \le X \le \mu + \sigma) \approx 0.6826\)
  • “Prob that \(X\) falls within 1 std dev of its mean is about 68%”

Important Properties (cont’d)

 

Important Properties (cont’d)

  • \(P(\mu - 2\sigma \le X \le \mu + 2\sigma) \approx 0.9544\)
  • “Prob that \(X\) falls within 2 std dev of its mean is about 95%”

Important Properties (cont’d)

 

Important Properties (cont’d)

  • \(P(\mu - 3\sigma \le X \le \mu + 3\sigma) \approx 0.9973\)
  • “Prob that \(X\) falls within 3 std dev of its mean is about 99%”

Important Properties (cont’d)

These will also come in handy:

  • \(P(\mu - 1.645 \sigma \le X \le \mu + 1.645 \sigma) \approx 0.90\)
  • \(P(\mu - 1.960 \sigma \le X \le \mu + 1.960 \sigma) \approx 0.95\)
  • \(P(\mu - 2.576 \sigma \le X \le \mu + 2.576 \sigma) \approx 0.99\)

Example: Standardized Testing

  • An erasure analysis detects and measures WTR (“wrong to right”) erasures

  • In controlled testing, the number of WTR’s is known to be normally distributed

  • In 2009, Georgia 3rd graders had a mean WTR of 1.87 on the state math test with std dev 0.6

  • However, one class at Dunbar Elementary in Atlanta had a mean WTR of 14.47, which statistically indicates cheating

  • From this type of evidence, widespread cheating was uncovered in Georgia in 2010

Calculating the Normal

  • Let \(X \sim N(\mu, \sigma)\) be a normal random variable
  • Let \(x\) be a value
    • \(P(X \le x) = \text{NORM.DIST}(x, \mu, \sigma, 1)\)
  • Let \(p\) be a probability
    • \(\text{NORM.INV}(p, \mu, \sigma)\) calculates the \(x\) such that \(P(X \le x) = p\)

Normal Example #1

Otis Elevator in Bloomington, Indiana, reported that the number of hours lost per week last year due to employees’ illnesses was approximately normally distributed, with a mean of 60 hours and a standard deviation of 15 hours. Determine, for a given week, the following probabilities:

  1. The number of hours lost will exceed 85 hours.

  2. The number of hours lost will be between 45 and 55 hours.

Normal Example #2

A mail-order company has estimated one-year demand for a popular item to be normally distributed with a mean of 180,000 units and a standard deviation of 15,000 units.

  1. What is the probability of selling all of the stock on hand if inventory equals 200,000 units?

  2. What inventory should the company have on hand if they want the probability of running out of stock to be 5%?

Calculating the Normal (cont’d)

The book discusses an alternative method:

  • Given \(X \sim N(\mu, \sigma)\), then \(Z = (X - \mu)/\sigma\) is normal with mean 0 and std dev 1, i.e., \(Z \sim N(0, 1)\)

  • \(Z\) is the standard normal

  • Can calculate \(P(X \le x) = P(Z \le (x - \mu)/\sigma) = \text{NORM.S.DIST}((x - \mu)/\sigma, 1)\)

Calculating the Normal (cont’d)

Why bother with this alternative method?

  • Especially before computers, people made “z tables” with all the \(P(Z \le z)\) values (see book Appendix)

  • A \(z\) table allows one to calculate any normal probability by switching to the standard normal

  • This alternative method also reflects the way people often work with the normal…

Standard Normal

  • Once you know a situation follows the normal distribution, it is common to talk about “\(z\)-values”

  • A \(z\)-value measures the number of std dev’s a values is from the mean, either below (negative \(z\)-value) or above (positive \(z\)-value)

Group Exercise #1

A certain business magazine recently reported that the average earnings per share for leading U.S. corporations is \(N(2.41,2.10)\).

  1. What proportion of corporations have earnings per share in excess of $6.00?

  2. What is the probability that earnings per share will be negative?

Group Exercise #2

Clerical workers process checks in batches of 1000. Historically, the number of errors per batch is normally distributed with mean 40 and std dev 6.

  1. Provide a 99% probability interval for the number of errors in any given batch, i.e., determine \(A\) and \(B\) (centered around the mean) such that \(P(A \le \text{errors} \le B)\) is approximately 99%.

  2. One worker had 60 errors in a single batch. This person claims that 60 errors is not unusual. Do you agree?
