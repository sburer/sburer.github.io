Binomial Distribution

The binomial distribution is one of the most commonly encountered DRVs. It has the following characteristics…

  1. One experiment is repeated \(n\) times

  2. In each experiment, only two outcomes are possible—either “success” or “failure”

  3. The experiments are independent, that is, knowing the outcome of one gives you no information about the outcomes of the others

  4. In each experiment, the probability of success is \(p\) (and so the probability of failure is \(1-p\))

  5. The random variable \(X\) is “number of successes in \(n\) trials”

Equivalently:

  1. One experiment is repeated \(n\) times; \(X_i\) represents \(i\)-th experiment

  2. \(X_i = 1\) or \(X_i = 0\)

  3. \(X_i\) is independent of \(X_j\)

  4. \(P(X_i = 1) = p\) and \(P(X_i = 0) = 1-p\)

  5. \(X = X_1 + X_2 + \cdots + X_n\)

Plinko Probability

  • The values of \(n\) and \(p\) are called the binomial parameters

  • If you think you have a binomial situation you must identify

    1. Fundamental success/failure experiment
    2. Value of \(n\)
    3. Value of \(p\)

Hockey example:

The Chicago Blackhawks score about 3.6 goals per (home) game. So they have a 1/1000 chance of scoring a goal at time 10:00 in the second period (since each game last 3600 seconds). If the Blackhawks play 41 home games, what is the probability that at least one such well-timed goal will occur during the season?

  • Fundamental success/failure experiment?
  • Values of \(n\) and \(p\)?

Binomial Probabilities

If you have a binomial situation with \(n\) and \(p\), the Excel formula for the probability of exactly \(k\) successes in \(n\) trials is:

# Successes Math Excel
exactly \(k\) \(P(X = k)\) BINOM.DIST(\(k\), \(n\), \(p\), 0)

Given \(n\) and \(p\), the Excel formula for the probability of at most \(k\) successes:

# Successes Math Excel
at most \(k\) \(P(X \le k)\) BINOM.DIST(\(k\), \(n\), \(p\), 1)

Important fact:

\[P(X > k) = P(X \ge k+1) = 1 - P(X \le k)\]

# Succ. Math Excel
at least \(k\) \(P(X \ge k)\) 1-BINOM.DIST(\(k\)-1, \(n\), \(p\), 1)
fewer than \(k\) \(P(X < k)\) BINOM.DIST(\(k\)-1, \(n\), \(p\), 1)
more than \(k\) \(P(X > k)\) 1-BINOM.DIST(\(k\), \(n\), \(p\), 1)

Binomial Mean and Std Dev

Suppose \(X\) is binomial with parameters \(n\) and \(p\):

\[E(X) = n \cdot p\] \[\sigma_X = \sqrt{n \cdot p \cdot (1 - p)}\]

  • Does the formula for \(E(X)\) make sense?
  • What happens to \(\sigma_X\) when \(p\) gets close to 0? Or close to 1?

Example: Churn

Analysts of the wireless industry estimate that, every month, 0.5% of all wireless customers churn, i.e., 0.5% of customers switch carriers

  • How many times per year does the average customer churn?

  • What proportion of customers are “high churners,” i.e., churn 2+ times per year?

  • For a given customer, let \(X\) be the number of times the customer churns in 12 months

  • What assumption is necessary to apply the binomial model? Is this a reasonable assumption?

  • Parameters n = 12 and p = 0.005

  • \(E(X) = n \cdot p = 12 \cdot 0.005 = 0.06\)

  • \(P(X \ge 2) = 1 - \text{BINOM.DIST}(1,12,0.005,1) =\) 0.16%

Example: Mud Slides

Suppose the chance of a mud slide occurring in a given rainy season is 1%. What is the chance of having at least one mud slide in the next 20 rainy seasons?

  • \(n = 20\), \(p = 0.01\), \(P(X \ge 1)\)
  • \(1 - \text{BINOM.DIST}(0, 20, 0.01, 1) \approx 0.182\)
